Overview

Reproduced and extended NeRF², a neural radiance field model adapted for RF signal propagation through physical environments. The original implementation was tightly coupled to CUDA, so the first phase was a ground-up portability pass that made the renderer, training loop, and model run correctly on Apple Silicon through the MPS backend.

The project then expanded past reproduction into two research directions: controlled synthetic benchmarking with NVIDIA Sionna and an efficiency study inspired by Instant-NGP. That turned the work from “can I rerun the paper?” into “what actually governs accuracy, generalization, and speed for radio NeRFs?”

What Was New
  • Reproduced the published BLE result on Apple Silicon and matched the paper at roughly 2.8 dBm median error
  • Fixed configuration and portability bugs, including transmitter positional encoding and non-CUDA execution paths
  • Built a full Sionna-to-NeRF² dataset pipeline using Blender scenes, Mitsuba export, and RF path solving at 2.4 GHz
  • Benchmarked generalization across concrete, metal, and wood walls while keeping geometry and sampling constant
  • Evaluated four Instant-NGP-style acceleration ideas: hash encoding, architecture reduction, occupancy-grid skipping, and fused Metal integration
  • Used the failure cases to explain where RF propagation diverges from image-rendering assumptions
Three Contributions
01
Reproduction on Apple Silicon
The baseline model trains and evaluates on MPS after fixing CUDA-specific assumptions and missing configuration pieces. The reproduced model reaches 2.88 dBm median error at 10k iterations, essentially matching the original paper while making the code usable on consumer Apple hardware.
02
Synthetic RF Benchmarking
A controlled benchmark suite was built with 6,000 transmitter positions and 21 gateways per scene, matching the structure of the real BLE dataset. This made it possible to vary only material properties and measure how NeRF² responds to physically different environments.
03
Acceleration Study
The work tests which Instant-NGP ideas transfer to radio. The answer is mixed: some techniques reduce cost, but several degrade accuracy or run into MPS dispatch overhead, showing that RF attenuation fields behave differently from visual NeRF workloads.
Sionna Pipeline
STEP 01
Scene Authoring
Indoor scenes were designed in Blender, exported through Mitsuba XML, and assigned electromagnetic material properties so the same geometry could simulate concrete, metal, or wood walls.
STEP 02
RF Simulation
NVIDIA Sionna solved RF propagation at 2.4 GHz with LOS, specular reflection, and refraction enabled. Path amplitudes were converted into RSSI in dBm and exported in NeRF² training format.
STEP 03
Model Evaluation
With geometry, TX/RX layout, and train/test split held constant, the benchmark isolated one physical factor at a time. Concrete and metal stayed near 2.1 MSE, while wood rose to 3.31 MSE because its weaker reflection and greater transmission create harder multipath structure.
Acceleration Findings
Hash Encoding
Vectorizing the encoder recovered much of the implementation overhead, but the best hash-only variant still trailed the sinusoidal baseline in accuracy. A smaller full “fast model” converged early, then plateaued far above baseline at long training horizons.
Smaller MLPs
Architecture reduction delivered real speedups, but the accuracy cliff was steep once the model became too small. A mid-sized slim network offered the best balance; aggressively tiny variants saved time at the cost of several dBm of error.
Occupancy and Metal
Occupancy-grid skipping only helped under certain voxel resolutions, and the fused Metal integration kernel delivered little gain on MPS because launch overhead replaced the dispatch overhead it was meant to remove. The lesson was architectural, not just implementation-level.

Takeaways

The project ended up being most valuable where it contradicted the easy story. Porting the code proved NeRF² is reproducible on Apple Silicon, but the efficiency work showed that radio is not just image NeRF with different labels. The representation is learning attenuation and multipath structure rather than color, and that changes which approximations are safe.

That is why the page focuses on both wins and limits: reproducing baseline performance, building a benchmark with controllable physics, and identifying where common NeRF acceleration tricks stop transferring cleanly. For a research project, those negative results were as useful as the positive ones.
